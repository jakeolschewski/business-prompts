## Sales & Lead Generation

### 1. Cold Email

**Use when:** You're reaching out to a prospect for the first time and want to open a conversation — not pitch too early.

**Prompt:**

> You are an expert B2B sales copywriter with 15+ years of experience writing cold emails that get replies.
>
> I need you to write a cold outreach email to a prospect I've never contacted before.
>
> **Context:**
> - My name: [Your Name]
> - My company: [Company Name]
> - What I do: [One sentence describing your product/service]
> - Prospect name: [First Name]
> - Prospect company: [Company Name]
> - Prospect role: [Job Title]
> - Why I'm reaching out to them specifically: [Personalization hook — recent news, mutual connection, their content, job posting, etc.]
> - The problem I solve for them: [Specific pain point relevant to their role]
> - Proof point: [One result, client win, or credential]
>
> **Deliverable:**
> A cold email under 120 words. Subject line included. Ends with a low-friction CTA (not "let's schedule a call" — something easier like a yes/no question or asking if the topic is relevant).
>
> **Constraints:**
> - No fluff opening lines ("Hope this finds you well", "I know you're busy")
> - Lead with the prospect's world, not mine
> - One idea per email — do not list services
> - Sound like a human, not a marketing department
> - No attachments or links in the first email
>
> **Before responding, evaluate:** Does the first sentence make the prospect feel seen? Would a skeptical executive keep reading past sentence two?

**Example output preview:**
> Subject: [Company]'s expansion into APAC
>
> Hi [First Name] — saw the announcement about your APAC launch. Most ops teams hit the same wall at that stage: localizing without blowing the support budget. We helped [Similar Company] cut ticket volume 40% during their expansion. Worth a quick conversation?

### 2. Follow-Up Email

**Use when:** You sent an initial email and got no response — you want to follow up without being annoying or desperate.

**Prompt:**

> You are a B2B sales coach who specializes in follow-up sequences that re-engage cold prospects without burning the relationship.
>
> I need a follow-up email for a prospect who hasn't responded to my first outreach.
>
> **Context:**
> - Original email was sent: [X days/weeks ago]
> - Original email topic: [Brief summary]
> - Prospect name: [First Name]
> - Prospect company: [Company]
> - Any new information since the first email: [New trigger, news, insight — or "none"]
>
> **Deliverable:**
> A follow-up email under 80 words that adds new value (doesn't just say "circling back"). Include a subject line.
>
> **Constraints:**
> - Never use "just following up" or "circling back"
> - Add at least one new piece of value, insight, or relevant news
> - Keep the tone confident, not apologetic
> - Give them an easy out ("if this isn't a fit, no worries") — it actually increases replies
>
> **Before responding, evaluate:** Does this email give them a reason to respond that wasn't in the first email?

**Example output preview:**
> Subject: One more thing on [topic]
>
> Hi [First Name] — meant to include this in my last note: [new insight, stat, or article]. Thought it was relevant given what you're building. If timing isn't right, totally understand — but if it is, happy to share how we've tackled this. Worth 15 minutes?

### 3. LinkedIn Connection Request

**Use when:** You want to connect with someone on LinkedIn and need a personalized note (not the default "I'd like to add you to my network").

**Prompt:**

> You are a LinkedIn outreach specialist who helps professionals build meaningful connections.
>
> Write a LinkedIn connection request note for me.
>
> **Context:**
> - My name: [Your Name]
> - My role: [Your Title]
> - Prospect name: [First Name]
> - Why I want to connect: [Genuine reason — their content, mutual interest, industry, specific post, event you both attended]
> - What I want from the connection long-term: [Collaboration, learning, business, networking]
>
> **Deliverable:**
> A connection request note under 300 characters (LinkedIn's limit). No pitch. Just a reason to connect.
>
> **Constraints:**
> - No selling in the connection request
> - Reference something specific to them (not generic)
> - Sound like a real person, not a script
> - End with something that makes them curious or want to accept
>
> **Before responding, evaluate:** Would I accept this connection request if someone sent it to me?

### 4. LinkedIn DM

**Use when:** You're already connected and want to start a genuine business conversation without coming off as spammy.

**Prompt:**

> You are a sales professional who excels at warm LinkedIn outreach that feels like a real conversation, not a pitch.
>
> Write a LinkedIn DM to send to someone I'm connected with.
>
> **Context:**
> - My name: [Your Name]
> - My company and offering: [Brief description]
> - Recipient's name: [First Name]
> - How we're connected: [Mutual connection, met at event, follow each other, engaged with content]
> - What triggered this message now: [Their post, job change, company news, something specific]
> - Goal of this message: [Start a conversation that could lead to a demo/meeting/partnership]
>
> **Deliverable:**
> A 3–5 sentence DM. Open with the trigger, offer value or curiosity, close with a soft question.
>
> **Constraints:**
> - No "I wanted to reach out about…" openers
> - No immediate pitch or calendar link in the first message
> - One focused idea only
> - Conversational, not corporate
>
> **Before responding, evaluate:** Does this DM feel like it was written for this specific person or could it be sent to 500 people?

### 5. Sales Call Script

**Use when:** You're preparing for a discovery call and want a structured framework to qualify the prospect and move toward a proposal.

**Prompt:**

> You are a sales trainer who has coached 200+ B2B sales reps and consultants. You specialize in discovery call frameworks that uncover real pain and create urgency without being pushy.
>
> Build me a discovery call script for an upcoming sales call.
>
> **Context:**
> - My company: [Company Name]
> - What I sell: [Product/service description]
> - Prospect: [Name, Title, Company]
> - Call duration: [30 / 45 / 60 minutes]
> - What I know about them already: [Research notes, pain points suspected, trigger for the call]
> - My goal for this call: [Qualify, set next step, close, etc.]
>
> **Deliverable:**
> A full call script with: (1) Opening & rapport, (2) Agenda-setting, (3) Discovery questions (5–8 questions), (4) Presenting relevance, (5) Handling common objections, (6) Next step close. Include timing guidance per section.
>
> **Constraints:**
> - Questions should uncover emotional and business pain, not just logistics
> - Include a talk-less-listen-more reminder
> - The close should offer a concrete next step, not "I'll send you some information"
> - Avoid jargon; this should work for a first-time founder as much as a seasoned rep
>
> **Before responding, evaluate:** Does this script create space for the prospect to talk more than the seller?

### 6. Objection Handling

**Use when:** You keep hearing the same objections and want scripted, professional responses to the most common ones.

**Prompt:**

> You are a B2B sales coach specializing in objection handling. You teach consultative responses that address the real concern behind an objection — not just deflect it.
>
> Generate objection handling responses for my business.
>
> **Context:**
> - My business: [Company/service description]
> - Target customer: [Who you sell to]
> - My price point: [Approximate range]
> - Top objections I hear: [List 3–5: "Too expensive", "We're using a competitor", "Not the right time", "Need to think about it", "Need to check with my boss", etc.]
>
> **Deliverable:**
> For each objection: (1) Acknowledge it genuinely, (2) Clarify the real concern with a question, (3) Reframe or respond with a specific point, (4) Suggest the next step. Format as a table or clearly labeled sections.
>
> **Constraints:**
> - Never argue with the prospect
> - Always acknowledge before responding
> - Responses should feel natural in spoken conversation
> - Include the clarifying question — most reps skip this and it's the most important part
>
> **Before responding, evaluate:** Does each response make the prospect feel heard before making a business case?

**Example output preview:**
> **"It's too expensive"**
> Acknowledge: "That's fair — I'd feel the same way without the full picture."
> Clarify: "Can I ask — is it that the number is outside budget entirely, or more that you're not sure it'll be worth it?"
> Reframe: "Most of our clients said the same before they saw the ROI breakdown. Can I show you what [Similar Company] got in 90 days?"

### 7. Proposal Email

**Use when:** You've completed a discovery call and are sending a proposal — you need an email that frames the proposal before they open the document.

**Prompt:**

> You are a business development consultant who has written thousands of successful proposal emails for consultants, agencies, and service providers.
>
> Write a proposal email to accompany a consulting proposal I'm sending.
>
> **Context:**
> - My name: [Your Name]
> - My company: [Company Name]
> - Prospect name: [First Name]
> - Company I'm proposing to: [Prospect Company]
> - The problem we discussed: [2–3 sentences on their pain]
> - The solution I'm proposing: [Brief description]
> - Proposal value/price range: [Optional — can be in the proposal doc itself]
> - What's attached: [Proposal title/document name]
> - Deadline or urgency: [If any]
>
> **Deliverable:**
> A proposal email under 200 words. Not a summary of the proposal — an email that creates anticipation and frames the proposal favorably before they open it.
>
> **Constraints:**
> - Reference the specific conversation you had
> - Don't recap everything — tease the solution
> - Include a clear CTA: review by [date] and reply with questions or a time to review together
> - Confident tone — not "I hope this meets your needs"
>
> **Before responding, evaluate:** Does this email make the prospect excited to open the attachment?

### 8. Pricing Justification

**Use when:** A prospect is pushing back on your price and you need to articulate your value clearly without caving on rate.

**Prompt:**

> You are a pricing strategist who helps consultants and service businesses communicate their value so that price becomes secondary to outcome.
>
> Help me write a pricing justification response for a prospect questioning my rate.
>
> **Context:**
> - My service: [What you do]
> - My price: [Your rate — hourly, project, retainer]
> - The objection I received: [Quote them if possible]
> - What I deliver: [Outcomes, results, deliverables]
> - Comparable alternatives they might consider: [DIY, cheaper competitor, hiring in-house]
> - A result I've delivered for someone similar: [Client win, metric, outcome]
>
> **Deliverable:**
> A 3–4 paragraph email or talking-points doc that:
> 1. Validates their concern
> 2. Reframes price as investment vs. cost
> 3. Shows the math of ROI
> 4. Closes with confidence, not desperation
>
> **Constraints:**
> - Never apologize for your price
> - Use real numbers and comparisons where possible
> - Leave room for a payment plan option without leading with it
>
> **Before responding, evaluate:** Would this response make a smart business owner reconsider their hesitation based on logic and evidence?

### 9. Win-Back Email

**Use when:** A deal went cold or a past client stopped working with you, and you want to re-engage professionally.

**Prompt:**

> You are a client retention specialist who helps businesses re-engage lost deals without being pushy or desperate.
>
> Write a win-back email to a prospect or past client I've lost touch with.
>
> **Context:**
> - Recipient: [Name, Company]
> - When we last spoke: [Timeframe]
> - Why the deal went cold or relationship ended: [Their reason, if known — budget, timing, competitor, etc.]
> - What has changed since then: [New offer, lower price, new case study, new capability, market change]
> - What I want them to do: [Reconnect, take a call, consider a new proposal]
>
> **Deliverable:**
> A win-back email under 150 words. Subject line included. Tone: warm, direct, confident. No groveling.
>
> **Constraints:**
> - Acknowledge the gap without over-explaining
> - Lead with what's new or changed — not "I wanted to check in"
> - Give them a specific reason to respond now
> - Respect their time and decision from before
>
> **Before responding, evaluate:** Is there a real reason for them to respond today, or is this just "touching base"?

### 10. Referral Ask

**Use when:** You have a happy client and want to ask for referrals in a way that feels natural and gets specific results.

**Prompt:**

> You are a business development consultant who specializes in building referral systems for service businesses.
>
> Write a referral request email for a satisfied client.
>
> **Context:**
> - Client name: [Name]
> - Project/service I delivered: [Description]
> - Results they got: [Specific outcome]
> - Type of referral I'm looking for: [Describe your ideal customer — role, company size, industry, situation]
> - Incentive (if any): [Discount, referral fee, gift card — or leave blank if none]
>
> **Deliverable:**
> A referral request email that: celebrates the result, makes the ask specific, makes it easy to refer (give them language to share), and expresses genuine appreciation.
>
> **Constraints:**
> - Be specific about who you want referred — "anyone who might benefit" is too vague
> - Give them an easy intro template so they don't have to think
> - Under 200 words
> - Warm and grateful tone — not transactional
>
> **Before responding, evaluate:** After reading this, would a happy client know exactly who to refer and how to do it?

**Example output preview:**
> "If you know any other [role] at [company type] who's dealing with [specific problem], I'd love an intro. Here's something you could forward: 'Hey — I've been working with [Your Name] on [X] and got [result]. Thought you might be interested.'"

### 11. Upsell Pitch

**Use when:** You're working with an existing client and want to propose additional services in a way that adds value, not just revenue.

**Prompt:**

> You are a client success strategist who helps consultants and agencies grow revenue from existing clients by identifying and communicating genuine expansion opportunities.
>
> Write an upsell pitch for an existing client.
>
> **Context:**
> - Client name: [Name, Company]
> - Current engagement: [What you're doing for them now]
> - Results delivered so far: [Metrics, milestones, outcomes]
> - Additional service I want to propose: [Description]
> - How the new service connects to what they're already experiencing: [The logical next step]
> - Value of the new service: [Outcome it will deliver]
>
> **Deliverable:**
> A 3-paragraph email or conversation talking points that: (1) references existing progress, (2) identifies the natural next problem, (3) proposes the solution with expected outcome. Includes a soft CTA.
>
> **Constraints:**
> - Frame as "the natural next step" not "an add-on"
> - Reference their specific progress — not generic praise
> - Don't oversell — let the logic carry it
>
> **Before responding, evaluate:** Does this feel like advice from a trusted advisor or a sales pitch?

### 12. Lead Qualification Questions

**Use when:** You need a structured set of discovery questions to quickly determine if a prospect is worth pursuing.

**Prompt:**

> You are a sales strategist who helps B2B founders and consultants qualify leads quickly so they don't waste time on bad-fit prospects.
>
> Generate a lead qualification question set for my business.
>
> **Context:**
> - My business: [What you do]
> - Ideal customer profile: [Company size, industry, role, situation]
> - Deal size/project range: [Your typical engagement]
> - Common reasons deals fall through: [Budget, authority, timeline, bad fit]
> - Qualifying criteria I care most about: [Budget, urgency, decision-making authority, problem severity]
>
> **Deliverable:**
> 10–15 qualification questions organized by BANT (Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline) plus a "Fit" category. For each, note what a good answer looks like vs. a red flag answer.
>
> **Constraints:**
> - Questions should feel conversational, not like a form
> - Include both direct and indirect questions (some people won't answer direct budget questions)
> - Identify the 3 "knockout" questions — if they fail these, end the call early
>
> **Before responding, evaluate:** Would these questions reliably separate ideal clients from time-wasters within 20 minutes?

## Client Management

### 1. Client Onboarding Welcome

**Use when:** A new client just signed and you want to kick off the relationship with a professional, warm welcome that sets expectations.

**Prompt:**

> You are a client experience specialist who helps service businesses create onboarding experiences that reduce churn and build trust from day one.
>
> Write a new client welcome email.
>
> **Context:**
> - My name: [Your Name]
> - My company: [Company Name]
> - Client name: [First Name]
> - Client company: [Company]
> - Service they signed for: [Description]
> - Start date: [Date]
> - What happens next: [Kickoff call, intake form, access to portal, etc.]
> - Key contact on my team (if applicable): [Name, role]
> - One thing that makes working with me different: [Your differentiator]
>
> **Deliverable:**
> A welcome email that covers: (1) warm congratulations on starting, (2) what to expect in the first week, (3) clear next steps with dates/links, (4) how to reach me, (5) genuine excitement about the work ahead.
>
> **Constraints:**
> - Warm but professional — not overly effusive
> - Bullet points for next steps so they're scannable
> - Under 300 words
> - Ends with excitement, not a list of their obligations
>
> **Before responding, evaluate:** After reading this, would a new client feel excited, clear on what's next, and confident they made the right choice?

**Example output preview:**
> "Welcome to the team, [First Name]. I'm genuinely excited to get started on [project]. Here's what the next 7 days look like: [Day 1: Complete intake form (link), Day 3: Kickoff call (calendar link), Day 7: First deliverable review]. Questions before then? Hit reply anytime."

### 2. Project Kickoff Agenda

**Use when:** You're running a kickoff meeting for a new project and want a structured agenda that sets the tone for the engagement.

**Prompt:**

> You are a project management consultant who specializes in setting projects up for success from the first meeting.
>
> Create a project kickoff meeting agenda.
>
> **Context:**
> - Project name: [Project Name]
> - Client: [Company Name]
> - Meeting duration: [60 / 90 minutes]
> - Attendees: [List names/roles from both sides]
> - Project goals: [2–3 high-level objectives]
> - Known risks or sensitivities: [Anything to be aware of]

>
> **Deliverable:**
> A formatted meeting agenda with: time allotments per section, discussion questions for each item, who leads each section, and a clear list of decisions that must be made in this meeting.
>
> **Constraints:**
> - Leave 10 minutes at the end for open questions
> - Include relationship-building time, not just logistics
> - Make it clear what "done" looks like at the end of this meeting
> - Format should be easy to share via email in advance
>
> **Before responding, evaluate:** Would a client feel this meeting was worth their time and set the project up for success?

### 3. Weekly Status Update

**Use when:** You need to send a regular project update to a client that's professional, scannable, and builds confidence.

**Prompt:**

> You are a client communications specialist who helps agencies and consultants write status updates that clients actually read and appreciate.
>
> Write a weekly project status update email.
>
> **Context:**
> - Project: [Project Name]
> - Client: [Name, Company]
> - Week number or date range: [e.g., Week 3, Oct 14–18]
> - What was completed this week: [List of accomplishments]
> - What's in progress: [Current work]
> - What's coming next week: [Planned activities]
> - Any blockers or items needing client input: [List or "none"]
> - Overall project health: [On track / at risk / behind — and why]
>
> **Deliverable:**
> A status update email with clear sections: Completed, In Progress, Next Week, Blockers/Decisions Needed, and Overall Status. Use a simple RAG status indicator (Red/Amber/Green) for project health.
>
> **Constraints:**
> - Under 300 words — clients don't read essays
> - Lead with good news, then surface issues with solutions attached
> - Be specific — "completed homepage design" not "made progress on design"
> - Never bury a problem — surface it early with your plan to fix it
>
> **Before responding, evaluate:** Would a busy client who spends 90 seconds on this email know exactly where things stand?

### 4. Meeting Summary

**Use when:** You just finished a client meeting and need to send a professional recap with action items before end of day.

**Prompt:**

> You are an executive assistant and project coordinator who writes meeting summaries that prevent miscommunication and ensure accountability.
>
> Write a post-meeting summary email.
>
> **Context:**
> - Meeting name: [Meeting title]
> - Date: [Date]
> - Attendees: [Names and roles]
> - Topics discussed: [Bullet points or rough notes]
> - Decisions made: [List]
> - Action items: [Who does what by when]
> - Next meeting: [Date, if scheduled]
>
> **Deliverable:**
> A meeting summary email with: a 2-sentence overview, decisions made, action items table (Owner / Task / Due Date), and next steps.
>
> **Constraints:**
> - Send within 24 hours of the meeting (mention this is the case)
> - Neutral tone — summarize what was said, don't editorialize

> - The update: [What changed — new feature, price change, policy change, deprecation]
> - Why it's changing: [Business or product reason]
> - How it benefits customers: [The upside for them]
> - Effective date: [When the change takes effect]
> - What customers need to do (if anything): [Action required or optional]
> - Who is impacted: [All users / specific segment / new users only]
>
> **Deliverable:**
> An update announcement email with: a clear subject line naming the change, what's changing and when, why it's a good thing for them, what to do (if anything), and where to learn more or ask questions.
>
> **Constraints:**
> - Lead with the customer benefit — not the product team's excitement
> - If it's a price increase or negative change, be direct and explain why
> - Action items must be clear: "You don't need to do anything" or "Please [do X] by [date]"
> - Never bury a negative change in a "great news!" wrapper
>
> **Before responding, evaluate:** Would a customer reading this know exactly what's changing, when, and what (if anything) they need to do?

### 9. Churned Customer Survey

**Prompt:**

>
> Write a churned customer survey.
>

>
> **Deliverable:**
